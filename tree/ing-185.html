    description

    Triple sec is a branch of curaçao and a generic category for neutral spirit-based refined colorless orange-flavored liqueurs imitative of Cointreau. (Cointreau itself was originally labeled “Triple Sec Curaçao” before trademark concerns led the owners to focus entirely on their brand name.) Some 19th century bar guides appear to use the term “white curacao” to refer to triple sec.

    The principle distinction of triple sec relative to other curaçaos is that the base is a neutral conveyance for a very clean, sophisticated orange flavor, unattainable before the invention of the continuous still. Cheap triple secs usually fall short on sophisticatation.

    Commentary

    Triple sec is little more than orange peels steeped in neutral spirits then distilled into an essence and diluted with sugar and water. There are only four ingredients: ethyl alcohol, water, sugar and orange peels. Good triple sec generally “louches” when you add water (much as absinthe and ouzo do), because copious essential oils precipitate out of the alcohol into an emulsion. Quality control is everything, which is precisely why Cointreau came to define and dominate this category. There are other good triple secs, but Cointreau is the benchmark.
